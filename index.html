What is disability?

  • Is it something wrong with an individual?
  • Something to be treated or "fixed"?

And how do disabled people live?

  • Do they wish they didn't have the disability?
  • How in the world can they live independently?

If you said yes, those are common thoughts to have.

However, they reflect notions of

ableism.

To be honest, I've had my share of ableist beliefs myself.

However, that started to change when I took this class and watched this video:

I was confused.

I'd thought before that while people with disabilities should be treated as equals to non-disabled people, disability itself was overall a negative experience.

But Stella Young saw disability as inherently neutral, and called herself disabled, not "special-needs" or anything like that.

“[People say disability is] a bad thing, and to live with a disability makes you exceptional.

It's not a bad thing, and it doesn't make you exceptional.”

— Stella Young

So what is ableism?

Common ableist beliefs include:

  • People with disabilities should be segregated from others when learning.
  • People with disabilities will be poor.
  • People with disabilities may be homeless.
  • People with disabilities may need to be institutionalized.

However, there are a few key beliefs behind ableism:

  • People with disabilities have inherently less desirable lives.
  • By extension, disability itself is undesirable.
  • Coupled with this is the belief that disability occurs only on the individual level.

These beliefs turn into actions.

  • Accommodations for the disabled aren't made—ramps, for example.
  • Schools are often segregated by disability and the special-needs classroom.
  • Being dependent is seen as undesirable.
  • People often believe that those who take care of people with disabilities are "saints".
  • Prenatal genetic testing reinforces the belief that a disabled child is a tragedy.
  • Words for disablement are used in a derogatory manner—"lame excuse", "moron", etc.

This blatant discrimination is wrong.

How do you fight ableism?

First, understand disability.

Understand how people with disabilities work and live.

Recognize that there are a range of ways to live a fulfilling and valued life.

Understand how people are pressured to be "normal" in order to fit in.

Learn from the perspective of people with disabilities.

But what is disability, really?

The SOCIAL MODEL of disability says that disablement occurs when people with impairments are denied access to certain human experiences or institutions.

These can include:

  • Marriage
  • A Comprehensive Education
  • Access to buildings
  • Having a family (those with disabilities have been forcibly sterilized in the past)

People are naturally diverse.

Just like skin color or height, disability is a natural part of human variation.

Disability isn't inherently a bad experience.

Think of it this way: If society was designed around people who had wings and could fly, and schools were only located in the sky, a nondisabled person in our society couldn't receive an education without outside help.

Same goes for a person who can't enter buildings because there aren't any ramps, or a person who is removed from class because they can't sit still when learning.

Second, contribute to a cultural change.

One way to start is by using the correct language.

  • Just say the word—"disability" or the specific condition (e.g. autism) instead of "special-needs" or "handicapped".
    The goal is to remove the stigma from disability.
  • "Nondisabled" over "Normal"—Disability is "normal" for those experiencing it
  • "Uses a wheelchair" over "wheelchair-bound"—"wheelchair-bound" stigmatizes disability by portraying it as a limitation and thus inherently worse.

For a general rule of thumb, focus on their experiences, not their impairments.

Finally, take action.

Join a disability rights organization.

Advocate for policies in both govenment and the workplace that improve disabled lives.

Call attention to ableism in daily life.
